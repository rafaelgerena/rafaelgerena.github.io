Christian Klein’s Son Test: Why SAP’s CEO Made an AI Claim He Can’t Walk Back

By Rafael Gerena · July 17, 2026

Summary: SAP cut nearly 10,000 jobs in a 2024 restructuring without disclosing how many were tied to artificial intelligence, while adding more than 3,500 jobs on net since 2023, including new “forward-deployed engineer” roles. Christian Klein, SAP’s chief executive, told The New York Times he is no longer certain he would advise his own son to become a computer engineer, a reversal from his position two years earlier. Klein’s statement stands out because it is specific and attributable, unlike the hedged language most executives use when discussing unproven AI strategies. The claim carries personal risk: if SAP’s AI-driven workforce transition fails, Klein’s public advice to his son fails with it.

SAP cut nearly 10,000 jobs in a 2024 restructuring, and did not say how many were tied to AI. SAP says it has added more than 3,500 jobs on net since 2023, including roles under the title “forward-deployed engineer” that put engineers in direct contact with customers.

Christian Klein, the chief executive, told The New York Times he is no longer certain he would advise his own son to become a computer engineer, only that he would point him toward work that puts him in front of customers, working alongside AI. Two years ago, he says, he would have made the opposite recommendation.

Most executives making a public case for an unproven AI strategy stick to language that can’t be checked against results: aspirational, hedged, safe to walk back if the numbers don’t hold. Klein’s version is checkable. He named a preference for his own son, on the record, before knowing whether the transition he is overseeing will work.

That’s where the risk actually sits. A hedge costs a leader nothing if the transition fails. A specific claim does. Leaders willing to take that risk are the ones who earn credit for handling it well.
