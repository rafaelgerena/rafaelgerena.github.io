The Cloudflare Framework: Why Senior Communications Is a Seller, Not a Measurer

By Rafael Gerena · May 25, 2026

Originally published on LinkedIn, May 25, 2026.

Matthew Prince just laid off 20% of Cloudflare during record revenue growth. His framework for who stays and who goes is worth reading carefully if you work in communications.

Prince reaches back to Peter Drucker's 1954 "The Practice of Management" to sort every employee into one of three categories: builders create products, sellers sell those products, and measurers do everything else: operations, compliance, middle management, finance, internal audit. His argument: AI is coming for measurers, not builders or sellers.

Then he names marketing specifically. "Like in most companies," he writes, it was "teeming with measurers."

He's right about marketing operations. He's describing a real phenomenon: teams built around internal process, reporting, and coordination rather than external value creation. That work is automatable and vulnerable.

But senior external communications is not that function.

Prince defines sellers precisely: people who "build trust and fix whatever goes wrong." That is the job description of a VP of Communications or Chief Communications Officer. Not metaphorically. Literally.

As VP of Communications at Per Scholas, a national leader in AI and tech workforce development, that was my work. Here is what it produced.

A Christmas Day New York Times editorial board endorsement of Per Scholas as a model for workforce development. A front-page New York Times business section feature shaping how national audiences understood AI's impact on workers. A Harvard Business School podcast placing the Per Scholas CEO in conversation with one of the country's leading researchers on the future of work.

None of that is measuring. All of it is selling, in the highest sense of the word.

The communications roles that fall into Prince's measurer category: coordinators, schedulers, reporting analysts. The people who work hard and honorably in those roles deserve acknowledgment. What is changing is not their effort or their character. It is what organizations need from the communications function in an AI-driven economy.

The roles that are defensible are the ones that drive external credibility, institutional trust, and stakeholder relationships that no AI system can generate on its own.

Drucker was right in 1954. Prince is right about what drives organizational value in 2026.
