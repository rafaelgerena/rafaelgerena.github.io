The 88% Standard: What AI Role-Modeling Actually Looks Like in Senior Leadership

By Rafael Gerena · May 20, 2026

Summary: At organizations leading in AI adoption, 88% of managers visibly role-model AI use daily. At lagging organizations, the number is 25%. According to BCG's February 2026 research, that behavioral gap — not tools, budget, or strategy — is the leading indicator of whether an organization captures substantial value from AI.

Originally published on LinkedIn, May 20, 2026.

At companies leading in AI, 88% of managers actively role-model AI use in daily operations. At lagging orgs, the number is 25%.

That gap is not a technology gap. It is a human-behavior gap. And according to Boston Consulting Group's (BCG) February 2026 research on AI and workforce transformation, it is the leading indicator of whether an organization captures substantial value from AI. Not the tools, not the budget, not the strategy document.

The implication is precise: employees do not adopt AI because their organization mandates it. They adopt it because their direct managers use it visibly, every day.

I built a custom GPT for newsletter production at Per Scholas before anyone asked me to. I diagnosed and resolved a token consumption problem in a Claude API automation I developed for personalized outreach. I built GEO capability across four platforms while the practice was still unnamed in most communications functions.

None of that was assigned. All of it was visible.

BCG's research frames this as role-modeling. I think of it differently: every AI workflow a senior leader builds in production is a signal to their team about what is sanctioned and worth learning.

The 88% is not a benchmark to aspire to. It is a description of what leadership looks like when AI stops being a project and starts being how work gets done.

The question for every senior communications leader is not whether your organization has an AI strategy. It is whether your behavior makes that strategy credible to the people who have to execute it.
