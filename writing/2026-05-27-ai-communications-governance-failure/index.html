Ungoverned AI Communications Is a Governance Failure: How Language Shapes AI Adoption Before Policy Can Catch Up

By Rafael Gerena · May 27, 2026

Summary: Standard Chartered CEO Bill Winters apologized publicly after language describing workers in economic terms backfired internally and externally. Separately, prominent AI industry figures including Elon Musk and Larry Ellison have used reductive framing in public settings, describing people as less capable than the systems they are building. Both represent governance failures, not communications failures in isolation. According to a May 2026 YouGov/Economist poll, 71 percent of Americans believe AI is moving too fast, making undisciplined executive language a material threat to AI adoption. Organizations that govern how AI is discussed across every level of leadership will hold credibility advantages that technology alone cannot provide.

Originally published on LinkedIn, May 27, 2026.

Two different kinds of AI communications failure. Both preventable.

This month, Standard Chartered CEO Bill Winters apologized after his attempt to explain comments about replacing "lower-value human capital" with AI investment backfired publicly. Meanwhile, some of the most prominent voices in the AI industry, including Elon Musk and Larry Ellison, have publicly described people as "meat computers," less capable than the systems they are building.

Both were communications failures. Both were also governance failures. The second is what made the first inevitable.

The Standard Chartered language made it out of the building because no one at the strategy level was thinking about how it lands with employees whose jobs, careers, and economic security are directly at stake. The "meat computers" framing made it onto public stages because no one was thinking about how it lands with the people who will ultimately decide whether AI succeeds or fails in their organizations.

In these cases, the communication accelerated the problem it was supposed to solve. Standard Chartered's framing made a difficult workforce moment harder. The "meat computers" language is making AI adoption harder at exactly the moment when public concern about AI is running high, when 71% of Americans say the technology is moving too fast, and when graduates are booing commencement speakers who tell them to embrace it.

This is what ungoverned AI communications looks like at scale. And it is happening at the top of the industry.

The organizations that will navigate this moment with credibility intact are not the ones with the best AI technology. They are the ones that govern how AI is talked about internally, externally, and at every level of leadership where language shapes perception before policy has a chance to catch up.

That is not a soft skill. It is the function that keeps the other functions from compounding their own problems.
