Commencement Booing and AI Messaging: Why Executives Keep Getting the Audience Wrong

By Rafael Gerena · May 29, 2026

Originally published on LinkedIn, May 29, 2026.

At a May commencement ceremony for Glendale Community College in Arizona, the school's new AI-powered system malfunctioned, misreading or skipping many graduates' names as they walked across the stage. The room filled with boos.

That moment tells the graduation season story more clearly than any viral video of an executive getting jeered.

Across campuses this spring, commencement speakers have been met with booing after praising AI or urging graduates to accept it. Former Google CEO Eric Schmidt at the University of Arizona. Gloria Caulfield, a real estate executive, at the University of Central Florida. Scott Borchetta, founder of Big Machine Records, at Middle Tennessee State. The pattern is consistent and the coverage has been widespread.

But the coverage is missing the actual story.

The graduates are not simply anti-AI. Polling shows Gen Z uses AI frequently. Gallup found 51% of 14- to 29-year-olds use generative AI daily or weekly. The same Gallup report found that even the most engaged AI users are less positive about the technology than they were a year ago. What they are against is a specific kind of communication: executives telling people facing an uncertain job market, many of them carrying heavy student debt, to enthusiastically embrace the technology many fear will shrink entry-level opportunities.

Schmidt told the room: "When someone offers you a seat on a rocket ship, you don't ask which seat. You just get on." His speech was booed nearly without interruption for minutes on end. The inversion was immediate. These graduates are being forced onto the rocket ship. But there are not enough seats for all of them.

This is a communications problem before it is anything else. The executives who got booed made the same mistake. They led with technological inevitability instead of human outcomes. They optimized their message for the boardroom and delivered it to people living the consequences.

I spent two years as VP of Communications at Per Scholas, a national leader in AI and tech workforce development. The approach that worked was not enthusiasm for the technology. It was specificity about the human outcome: a former police officer who became a Microsoft data center technician. A graduate earning $125,000 without a four-year degree.

The story that lands is not "AI is the future." It is "here is a person whose life got better."

The executives who got booed this spring were not wrong about AI. They were wrong about how to talk about it. That is a communications failure.
