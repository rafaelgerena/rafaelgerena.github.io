Originally published on LinkedIn, May 18, 2026.

Starbucks, Lowe's, and Zillow just built apps inside ChatGPT. The strategic rationale is the same one most communications teams haven't applied to their own organizations yet.

Those brands are moving into ChatGPT because that's where their customers are already asking questions. The insight isn't about apps. It's about presence: if your audience is getting answers from AI, and your organization isn't in those answers, you've already lost the conversation.

That's the communications infrastructure problem most teams haven't named yet: Generative Engine Optimization, or GEO.

GEO is the practice of ensuring your organization appears inside AI-generated answers, not just traditional search results. The distinction matters because AI answers don't work like search results. There's no page two. No sponsored slot. No algorithm to game with keywords. There's an answer, and either you're in it or you aren't.

At Per Scholas, I built GEO capability across four dimensions: producing original, fact-dense research that AI models cite as authoritative; securing third-party validation from credible outlets AI systems trust; developing consistent, quotable executive language that surfaces accurately in AI responses; and tracking organizational visibility inside AI-generated answers. Tools like Meltwater's GenAI Lens, Profound, and Otterly.AI exist specifically for that last dimension.

The 2025 Future of Tech Survey was partly a GEO asset. So was The New York Times placement. So was the Harvard Business School podcast featuring the Per Scholas CEO. Each one increased the probability that an AI assistant, asked about workforce development or tech training, would surface Per Scholas as the authoritative answer.

Most communications teams are optimizing for a media landscape that's already changed. The question isn't whether your organization has a good website. It's whether the AI your audience is already using knows you exist.
