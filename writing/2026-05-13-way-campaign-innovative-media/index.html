The WAY Campaign: What 160 Million Viewers and a Concert Tour Teach About Innovative Media Strategy

Originally published on LinkedIn, May 13, 2026.

The biggest earned media opportunity in workforce development wasn't a press release. It was a concert tour.

Before cultural partnerships became a standard playbook move, Stand Together embedded the WAY campaign across 34 stops of the U.S. leg of The Weeknd's After Hours til Dawn tour. A WAY station at every tour stop asked fans a single question: Who are you? Over 1,800 people shared their stories. The campaign reached 160 million viewers.

At Per Scholas, I directed our communications team to coordinate content across coalition partners so each organization's amplification reinforced the others. Getting multiple mission-driven organizations to harmonize messaging across 34 tour stops without losing their individual voices is its own communications discipline. The reach was shared. The strategy had to be too.

What made the partnership work wasn't the celebrity association. It was narrative alignment. The Weeknd dropped out of high school at 17. Per Scholas trains people who skipped the traditional path. The WAY campaign, Who Are You, was the connective tissue. The message didn't need translation because it was already true.

This is what foresight-driven communications looks like in practice: identifying a cultural moment already carrying the audience you want to reach, then positioning your organization inside it before that moment becomes obvious.

Most nonprofit communications strategies aim at the audience they already have: journalists covering workforce development, policy contacts, institutional funders. Those channels don't reach the 71 million Americans held back from career growth because they don't have a degree. The WAY campaign inverted that logic entirely.

The strategic question worth sitting with: where is your audience's attention actually going in 2026? And is your organization positioned inside that current, or still sending press releases into a feed no one is reading?
