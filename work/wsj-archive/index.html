Wall Street Journal Economics Reporting Archive

Rafael Gerena

Summary: Before leading enterprise communications strategy, I was a staff economics reporter at The Wall Street Journal, covering national employment, inflation, consumer spending, federal policy, and real estate markets. This is the professional foundation for everything that followed: the ability to translate complex, high-stakes information into clear, credible narratives for sophisticated audiences, to understand how journalists evaluate sources and construct stories, and to write with the precision and economy that national publication standards require.

The Credential

The Wall Street Journal is among the most widely read and editorially rigorous publications in the world, with a global readership of business leaders, policymakers, investors, and senior executives. A staff byline at the Journal is not a credential that ages. It establishes a permanent baseline: this person can report, write, and think at the level the Journal's editors require.

Most communications executives pitch journalists. Fewer have been journalists. That structural difference matters when the work requires understanding how a national reporter evaluates a story, what evidence they need to include an organization, and how to position a source as credible rather than self-interested. It is the difference between media relations as a transaction and media relations as a craft.

What I Covered

My reporting at the Journal focused on economics and policy: national employment data and labor market trends, inflation and consumer spending, federal economic policy and its regional implications, and real estate market dynamics during a period of significant national volatility. The beat required translating Federal Reserve decisions, Bureau of Labor Statistics releases, and Congressional budget debates into stories that general business readers could understand and act on.

That translation discipline, taking complex, technical, or contested information and making it legible to a sophisticated but non-specialist audience, is the same discipline that runs through every communications role I have held since.

Why It Matters Now

Senior communications work in 2026 requires the same core skill the Journal required in 2005: the ability to identify what is actually significant in a complex situation, frame it accurately and compellingly, and deliver it to an audience that has limited time and high standards. The tools have changed. The discipline has not.

The Wall Street Journal years are also the reason the earned media results in the rest of this portfolio look the way they do. You do not secure a Christmas Day New York Times editorial board endorsement or shape a front-page Times technology feature without understanding how editors and reporters think. That understanding came from being one.
