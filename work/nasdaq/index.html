Nasdaq Closing Bell: Orchestrating a National Credibility Moment for Per Scholas

By Rafael Gerena

Summary: I orchestrated Per Scholas' appearance at the Nasdaq Closing Bell in February 2025, marking the organization's 30th anniversary. The event required coordinating 50+ cross-functional stakeholders across a high-visibility, zero-margin-for-error platform. It generated national media coverage across business and financial press and positioned Per Scholas with the financial services, investor, and corporate leadership community — an audience the organization's standard earned media strategy rarely reached directly.

Context

Per Scholas turned 30 in 2025. The anniversary represented a rare opportunity to shift how the organization was perceived by audiences beyond its existing base of workforce development stakeholders, policy contacts, and institutional funders. The Nasdaq Closing Bell offered a platform that carries a specific institutional signal: the financial services and corporate leadership community reads it as a marker of organizational legitimacy and national scale.

Earning that moment required more than securing the invitation. It required building the communications architecture around it — coordinating stakeholders, preparing leadership, managing media, and ensuring the event generated durable coverage rather than a single day of visibility.

The Approach

The Nasdaq Closing Bell is a high-visibility, zero-margin-for-error environment. Every element — leadership preparation, stakeholder coordination, media positioning, and post-event amplification — had to be planned and executed with precision across more than 50 cross-functional participants.

I led end-to-end planning and execution: securing the date, coordinating internal and external stakeholders, preparing organizational leadership for on-camera presence, managing media credentialing and outreach, and directing post-event coverage strategy to extend the moment beyond the ceremony itself.

The goal was not a single high-visibility moment. The goal was a reputational anchor — a verified, photographed, nationally covered institutional milestone that every subsequent corporate partnership conversation could reference.

Why It Matters

The Nasdaq ceremony reached an audience that workforce development organizations rarely access through standard earned media: financial services leadership, institutional investors, and corporate decision-makers who evaluate nonprofit partners on the basis of organizational credibility and national scale. The Closing Bell communicated both in a format that audience already understands.

Credibility moments like this one are not self-executing. They require a communications function that can identify the right platform, secure the access, coordinate the stakeholders, and build the amplification strategy that makes a single event earn sustained returns.
