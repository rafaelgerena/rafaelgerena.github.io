The WAY Campaign: Cross-Sector Partnership Across 34 Tour Stops and 160 Million Viewers

By Rafael Gerena

Summary: I directed Per Scholas communications inside the WAY campaign — a Stand Together initiative embedded across 34 stops of The Weeknd's After Hours til Dawn tour. The campaign reached 160 million viewers and required harmonizing messaging across a coalition of mission-driven organizations without losing individual voices. It is the largest-scale cultural partnership in Per Scholas history and a case study in foresight-driven communications: identifying a pop culture moment already carrying the audience you want to reach, then positioning your organization inside it before that moment becomes obvious.

Context

Per Scholas trains people who skipped the traditional path — adults seeking tech careers without four-year degrees. The organization's earned media strategy had historically targeted journalists covering workforce development, policy contacts, and institutional funders. Those channels do not reach the 71 million Americans held back from career growth because they lack a degree.

The WAY campaign inverted that logic. Rather than reaching the audience Per Scholas already had, it positioned the organization inside a cultural moment already carrying the audience Per Scholas needed to reach.

The Approach

Stand Together embedded the WAY campaign — Who Are You — across 34 stops of the U.S. leg of The Weeknd's After Hours til Dawn tour. A WAY station at every tour stop asked fans a single question: Who are you? Over 1,800 people shared their stories.

What made the partnership work was not the celebrity association. It was narrative alignment. The Weeknd dropped out of high school at 17. Per Scholas trains people who skipped the traditional path. The WAY campaign was the connective tissue. The message did not need translation because it was already true.

I directed the Per Scholas communications team to coordinate content across coalition partners so each organization's amplification reinforced the others. Getting multiple mission-driven organizations to harmonize messaging across 34 tour stops without losing their individual voices is its own communications discipline. The reach was shared. The strategy had to be too.

Why It Matters

This is what foresight-driven communications looks like at scale: identifying a cultural moment already carrying the audience you want to reach, then positioning your organization inside it before that moment becomes obvious to everyone else.

Most nonprofit communications strategies aim at the audience they already have. The WAY campaign demonstrated what becomes possible when a communications function identifies where its audience's attention is actually going — and builds presence there before the window closes.
